RETRIEVAL MECHANISMS

================================================================================
WHY IT MATTERS
================================================================================
Here's the thing about RAG: your AI is only as good as what it retrieves. Hand it irrelevant documents and even the smartest model will give garbage answers. Hand it exactly the right context and it looks like magic. Retrieval is where RAG applications succeed or fail. Mastering chunking, embeddings, hybrid search, and query optimization—these are the levers that turn 'meh' RAG into 'wow' RAG.

================================================================================
CONCEPT: Document Chunking Strategies
================================================================================
Chunking sounds boring until you realize it's where most RAG failures happen. Chunk wrong and your retrieval breaks—either missing relevant info or drowning the model in irrelevant context.

The fundamental tension: **precision vs. context**. Small chunks match queries precisely but might lack the surrounding context your model needs to understand them. Large chunks preserve context but contain so much irrelevant text that their semantic signal gets diluted. A 2000-token chunk about 'everything in Chapter 5' won't match as well as a focused chunk about 'exactly the paragraph answering the user's question.'

**Fixed-size chunking** is the blunt instrument: split every 512 tokens, done. Predictable, simple to implement, fits neatly into embedding model limits. But it might slice a sentence right in half. A chunk that starts with 'However, this approach fails when...' is useless without knowing what 'this approach' refers to.

**Semantic chunking** is smarter—it splits at natural boundaries like paragraph breaks and section headings. You get coherent chunks that stand alone. The trade-off? Sizes vary wildly. Some paragraphs are tiny; dense technical sections might exceed your limits.

**Hierarchical chunking** is the clever solution. Create parent chunks that summarize entire sections, and child chunks with the details. Search against parents to find relevant sections, then grab their children for specifics. You get both the forest and the trees.

**Overlapping chunks** add redundancy at boundaries. If your chunks are 512 tokens with 50-token overlap, adjacent chunks share content. When an answer spans a boundary, both chunks contain it. Better recall, more storage.

Bedrock KB handles chunking with configurable size and overlap. For custom pipelines, you build your own with Lambda. Either way, test different sizes with your actual data—there's no universal 'best' size.

KEY POINTS:
- Fixed-size: Simple and predictable, but may split mid-thought
- Semantic: Respects document structure, variable sizes
- Hierarchical: Parent summaries + child details for best of both
- Overlapping: Shared content at boundaries catches spanning answers

EXAM TIP: 'Context preservation' or 'parent-child' → hierarchical chunking. 'Simplest approach' → fixed-size with Bedrock KB defaults.

CONCEPT: Embedding Model Selection
================================================================================
Embeddings are the translation layer between human language and mathematical search. Pick a bad embedding model and your retrieval is doomed before it starts—similar meanings won't map to similar vectors.

**Amazon Titan Text Embeddings V2** is the AWS default with key improvements over V1. It produces **1024-dimensional vectors by default** (V1 was 1536—a ~33% storage reduction). But here's the clever part: V2 supports **flexible dimensions** (256, 512, or 1024). At 512 dims, you retain **99% retrieval accuracy**. At 256 dims, you still keep **97% accuracy**.

Example (TypeScript):
import { BedrockRuntimeClient, InvokeModelCommand } from '@aws-sdk/client-bedrock-runtime';

async function generateEmbedding(text: string, dimensions = 1024): Promise<number[]> {
  const client = new BedrockRuntimeClient({ region: 'us-east-1' });

  const response = await client.send(new InvokeModelCommand({
    modelId: 'amazon.titan-embed-text-v2:0',
    body: JSON.stringify({
      inputText: text,
      dimensions, // 256, 512, or 1024
      normalize: true
    }),
    contentType: 'application/json'
  }));

  const result = JSON.parse(new TextDecoder().decode(response.body));
  return result.embedding; // number[] of length 'dimensions'
}

V2 handles **100+ languages** and processes up to **8,192 tokens** (or 50,000 characters) per input.

**Cohere Embed** is available through Bedrock and shines for multilingual content. If your documents span multiple languages or users query in various languages, Cohere often outperforms Titan. It also ranks well on search-specific benchmarks. However, Cohere costs **~5x more** ($0.10/1M tokens vs Titan's $0.02/1M tokens).

**V2 strengths**: Handles minor spelling errors/variations better than competitors. Maintains semantic similarity even with noisy input. The flexible dimensionality is a game-changer for cost optimization.

**Dimensionality trade-off**: Higher dims = more semantic nuance but more storage/compute. With V2's dimension flexibility, you can start at 1024 and reduce if you need cost savings without re-embedding.

Here's the thing that trips people up: **domain matters**. An embedding model that crushes general benchmarks might struggle with your specific content. Medical terminology, legal jargon, technical docs—specialized domains can confuse general-purpose models. Evaluate with YOUR queries on YOUR documents before committing.

**Batch embedding** saves money at scale. Processing thousands of documents? Use batch inference instead of individual API calls. Lower cost, better throughput.

Critical rule: **all documents and all queries must use the same embedding model**. You can't embed docs with Titan and query with Cohere—the vector spaces are completely different. Switching models means re-embedding everything.

KEY POINTS:
- Titan V2: 1024 dims default (flexible: 256/512/1024), 100+ languages, 8K tokens
- Dimension flexibility: 512 dims = 99% accuracy; 256 dims = 97% accuracy
- Cost: Titan $0.02/1M tokens vs Cohere $0.10/1M tokens
- CRITICAL: Same embedding model for docs AND queries—always

EXAM TIP: Titan V2 default unless multilingual emphasis (→ Cohere). Know the dimension/accuracy tradeoffs: 512=99%, 256=97%. V2 handles 8,192 tokens max.

CONCEPT: Vector Search Techniques
================================================================================
Basic vector search finds documents whose embeddings are closest to your query embedding. Works great for semantic queries. But it has blind spots—it might miss relevant documents that use different words or include important keywords that embeddings don't capture well.

**Hybrid search** fixes this by combining vector similarity with old-school keyword matching (BM25). Think about it: if someone searches for error code 'E-1042', pure vector search might return docs about 'errors' generally. Hybrid search nails the exact match AND understands context. This combo often beats either approach alone.

Bedrock KB supports **HYBRID retrieval mode**—enable it via the Retrieve and RetrieveAndGenerate APIs:

import { BedrockAgentRuntimeClient, RetrieveCommand } from '@aws-sdk/client-bedrock-agent-runtime';

const client = new BedrockAgentRuntimeClient({});

const response = await client.send(new RetrieveCommand({
  knowledgeBaseId: 'KB12345',
  retrievalQuery: { text: 'How do I handle rate limiting?' },
  retrievalConfiguration: {
    vectorSearchConfiguration: {
      numberOfResults: 10,
      overrideSearchType: 'HYBRID' // Combines semantic + keyword
    }
  }
}));

// Results include relevance scores
response.retrievalResults?.forEach(result => {
  console.log(result.content?.text, result.score);
});

**Reranking** is the secret weapon for high-stakes retrieval. Bedrock provides the **Rerank API** with two models: **Amazon Rerank 1.0** and **Cohere Rerank 3.5**. Here's the pattern: **retrieve wide, rerank narrow**. First, vector search grabs top-N candidates (say, 20). Then the reranker scores each query-document pair and reorders them. Return top-K (say, 5). The reranker is slower but much more accurate—cross-encoders see query AND document together, catching relevance signals that separate embeddings miss. This consistently outperforms just retrieving 5 directly.

**MMR (Maximal Marginal Relevance)** solves the redundancy problem. Without it, you might get five chunks from the same document all saying the same thing. MMR penalizes redundancy, selecting results that are both relevant AND diverse. When your context window is limited, diversity matters.

**Filtering** constrains results by metadata. Pre-filtering (before search) is efficient—search a smaller pile. Post-filtering (after search) is simpler but might return too few results. Most production systems pre-filter on common constraints like document type or access level.

**Production pattern**: HIERARCHICAL chunking + HYBRID search + reranking. This is the most robust default for production RAG.

KEY POINTS:
- Hybrid search: BM25 keywords + vector similarity—enable via HYBRID retrieval mode
- Rerank API: Amazon Rerank 1.0 or Cohere Rerank 3.5 models
- Pattern: 'Retrieve wide, rerank narrow'—get 20, rerank to top 5
- Production default: Hierarchical + Hybrid + Reranking

EXAM TIP: 'Improve relevance' or 'precision' → Rerank API with Amazon Rerank 1.0 or Cohere Rerank 3.5. 'Retrieve wide, rerank narrow' outperforms direct retrieval.

CONCEPT: Query Handling Techniques
================================================================================
Users type messy queries. They're ambiguous, use different words than your documents, and sometimes ask three questions at once. Query optimization bridges this gap between what users type and what documents contain.

**Query expansion** adds related terms to catch more relevant docs. User asks about 'AWS compute options'? Expand to include 'EC2,' 'Lambda,' 'Fargate,' 'ECS.' Documents might use any of these terms. You can build synonym dictionaries or ask an FM to suggest related terms.

**Query decomposition** handles complex multi-part questions. 'Compare the cost and performance of EC2 vs Lambda for batch processing' is really four questions: EC2 cost, Lambda cost, EC2 performance, Lambda performance. Break it apart, retrieve for each sub-query, combine results. The FM gets all the relevant context instead of a muddled mix.

**HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings)** is a clever trick for difficult queries. Instead of embedding the query directly, ask an FM to write a hypothetical answer document. Then embed THAT and use it for retrieval. Why does this work? The hypothetical document is written in 'document style,' matching how your actual documents are written much better than a terse query.

**Query reformulation** cleans up messy input. Users type 'how 2 setup vpc peering???'—an FM can turn that into a proper query that retrieves better.

**Conversational context** matters for multi-turn chats. 'What about the pricing?' means nothing without knowing what 'that' refers to from earlier. Include relevant conversation history when building retrieval queries.

Step Functions can orchestrate complex query pipelines that expand, decompose, retrieve, rerank, and combine—all with proper error handling.

KEY POINTS:
- Query expansion: Add synonyms and related terms for better recall
- Query decomposition: Break complex questions into simpler sub-queries
- HyDE: Generate hypothetical answer document for better retrieval
- Reformulation: Clean up messy, ambiguous user queries

SKILL 1.5.1: Document Segmentation
================================================================================
Document segmentation determines how granular your retrieval can be. Get it right and you retrieve exactly what's needed. Get it wrong and you either miss relevant content or drown the model in noise.

For **structured documents** with clear hierarchies (manuals, legal docs, technical specs), semantic chunking works well. Split at section boundaries. Use headings as metadata for filtering.

For **unstructured content** (emails, chat logs, transcripts), fixed-size chunking with overlap may be better since natural boundaries are less clear.

Bedrock Knowledge Bases provides automatic chunking with configurable parameters. Set target chunk size (around 300 tokens is a good starting point) and overlap percentage (10% is common). Adjust based on retrieval quality with your actual queries.

Custom chunking pipelines offer more control. Lambda functions triggered by S3 uploads can implement domain-specific logic—split code by function, legal docs by clause, procedures by step. More work, better results for specialized content.

AWS SERVICES:
- Amazon Bedrock Knowledge Bases: Automatic chunking with configurable size and overlap. Handles most document types.
  Keywords: automatic chunking, managed, configurable size
- AWS Lambda: Custom chunking for specialized content. Semantic boundary detection with domain knowledge.
  Keywords: custom chunking, semantic, boundary detection

================================================================================
SKILL 1.5.2: Embedding Generation
================================================================================
Embedding generation turns your documents into the vectors that enable semantic search. Quality embeddings are foundational—no amount of fancy search can fix bad embeddings.

**Titan Embeddings V2** is the go-to for Bedrock workloads. Good English performance, improving multilingual support. For most use cases, just use it and move on.

Before going to production, **evaluate embedding quality** on your specific domain. Create a test set: queries with known relevant documents. Generate embeddings and measure recall@k—how many relevant docs appear in top k results? Compare embedding models on YOUR data; benchmarks don't always predict domain-specific performance.

**Batch embedding** is essential at scale. Processing thousands of documents with individual API calls is slow and expensive. Use Bedrock batch inference or batch your own calls for efficiency.

**Cache embeddings** to avoid recomputing. Store embeddings alongside document IDs. When documents update, only re-embed what changed. This matters a lot for incremental knowledge base updates.

AWS SERVICES:
- Amazon Titan Embeddings: Default embedding model for Bedrock. 1536 dimensions, solid general-purpose.
  Keywords: Titan, embeddings, 1536 dimensions, default
- Amazon Bedrock: Access multiple embedding models including Cohere. Batch inference for scale.
  Keywords: embedding model, batch inference, Cohere

================================================================================
SKILL 1.5.3: Vector Search Implementation
================================================================================
Vector search is where retrieval rubber meets the road. Implementation details significantly impact both quality and performance.

**OpenSearch k-NN** provides scalable vector search with HNSW indexes. HNSW finds approximate nearest neighbors incredibly fast—it may not be mathematically perfect, but it's close enough and way faster than exact search. For most applications, this trade-off is exactly right.

**Aurora pgvector** adds vector operations to PostgreSQL. If you already use PostgreSQL, querying vectors with familiar SQL is a big win. pgvector supports both exact search (accurate but slow at scale) and approximate search (IVFFlat indexes).

**Bedrock Knowledge Bases** abstracts the search layer completely. You call the retrieve API; Bedrock handles vector search internally. Simplest option, least control.

**Tuning search parameters** affects precision-recall trade-offs. In OpenSearch, the ef_search parameter controls how many candidates HNSW considers. Higher values = better recall, slower search. Test different values with your actual queries to find the balance.

AWS SERVICES:
- Amazon OpenSearch Service: Large-scale k-NN with HNSW. Fine control over search parameters and sharding.
  Keywords: k-NN, vector search, HNSW, scale
- Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL: Vector search for PostgreSQL shops. SQL-compatible queries with pgvector.
  Keywords: pgvector, PostgreSQL, SQL, IVFFlat
- Amazon Bedrock Knowledge Bases: Managed vector search, zero configuration. Simplest for most RAG apps.
  Keywords: managed, knowledge base, retrieve API

================================================================================
SKILL 1.5.4: Advanced Search Techniques
================================================================================
Advanced search techniques squeeze more precision out of retrieval. Worth the complexity for high-stakes applications where accuracy justifies latency.

**Hybrid search fusion** combines vector and keyword scores. OpenSearch's neural plugin lets you tune weights—emphasize keywords for precise technical queries, semantics for conceptual questions. Bedrock KB's HYBRID mode does this automatically with sensible defaults.

**Reranker models** score query-document pairs with more computational power than initial retrieval. Pattern: retrieve top-N quickly with vector search, then rerank those N documents with a more accurate (slower) cross-encoder, return top-K. Bedrock offers reranker models you can apply to retrieval results.

**Cross-encoder reranking** is powerful because it jointly processes query and document, catching relevance signals that separate embeddings miss. The downside? Can't be pre-computed—must run at query time. Use for precision-critical applications where extra latency is acceptable.

AWS SERVICES:
- Amazon OpenSearch Service: Neural plugin for hybrid search. Custom scoring. BM25 + vector fusion.
  Keywords: hybrid search, neural plugin, BM25, fusion
- Amazon Bedrock: Reranker models for result reordering. HYBRID retrieval mode for KB queries.
  Keywords: reranker, HYBRID mode, reorder, relevance

================================================================================
SKILL 1.5.5: Query Handling
================================================================================
Query handling transforms messy user input into effective retrieval queries. The gap between how users ask and how documents are written is where retrieval often fails.

**Query expansion** uses an FM to add related terms. 'Serverless compute' expands to include 'Lambda,' 'Fargate,' 'event-driven'—catching docs that use different terminology. Improves recall.

**Query decomposition** breaks complex questions into sub-queries. Essential for analytical questions spanning multiple topics. Retrieve for each, aggregate results. The FM gets comprehensive context instead of partial matches.

**Step Functions** orchestrates complex query pipelines. A sophisticated system might: (1) reformulate for clarity, (2) expand with related terms, (3) decompose into sub-queries, (4) retrieve for each, (5) rerank combined results, (6) apply MMR for diversity. Step Functions manages this multi-stage workflow with proper error handling.

**Conversational query handling** requires context awareness. In multi-turn chats, 'what about that?' references earlier context. Include relevant conversation history when constructing retrieval queries.

AWS SERVICES:
- Amazon Bedrock: FM-based query expansion, reformulation, decomposition. Generate hypothetical docs for HyDE.
  Keywords: query expansion, reformulation, HyDE, decomposition
- AWS Step Functions: Orchestrate multi-stage query pipelines. Handle complex branching and errors.
  Keywords: orchestrate, multi-stage, workflow, pipeline

================================================================================
SKILL 1.5.6: Access Mechanisms
================================================================================
Access mechanisms provide standardized interfaces between FMs and retrieval systems. These interfaces enable tool use and agentic patterns where the model decides when and how to retrieve.

**Function calling** lets FMs invoke retrieval tools. Define a retrieval function with parameters (query, filters, top_k), and the FM calls it when it needs external information. More flexible than always-retrieve approaches.

Bedrock's **Converse API** supports tools natively. Define tool schemas, and the FM returns tool_use blocks when it wants to invoke a tool. Your application executes the tool and returns results for the FM to incorporate.

**Lambda functions** serve as flexible API endpoints for retrieval. The FM invokes Lambda via tool calling, Lambda performs the retrieval (querying OpenSearch, Aurora, or Bedrock KB), and returns results to the FM.

**Model Context Protocol (MCP)** is an emerging standard for tool integration. Provides a consistent interface for FMs to interact with external tools and data sources—potentially simplifying multi-tool architectures.

AWS SERVICES:
- Amazon Bedrock: Converse API with tool definitions. Function calling for retrieval integration.
  Keywords: function calling, tools, Converse API, tool_use
- AWS Lambda: Custom retrieval APIs invoked via function calling. Bridge FM tool calls to data sources.
  Keywords: API, custom retrieval, tool execution

SERVICE COMPARISON: Search Technique Selection
================================================================================
| Criterion       | Pure Vector Search              | Hybrid Search                          | Hybrid + Reranking                   |
|-----------------|--------------------------------|----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Best for        | Conceptual, semantic queries   | Mixed queries with keywords + concepts | Precision-critical, complex queries  |
| Precision level | Good for semantic similarity   | Better—captures keywords vector misses | Highest—cross-encoder most accurate  |
| Latency impact  | Lowest latency                 | Slightly higher (two search types)     | Highest (two-stage retrieval)        |
| When to use     | Latency-critical, simple match | Most production RAG (default choice)   | High-stakes where accuracy > latency |

================================================================================
EXAM STRATEGIES
================================================================================
Pattern: "improve retrieval quality"
Approach: Look for hybrid search, reranking, or query expansion. These directly improve precision. Order of impact: hybrid search > reranking > query expansion. If question says 'without increasing latency,' favor hybrid over reranking.
Keywords: improve, quality, relevance, precision, better retrieval

Pattern: "complex or multi-part questions"
Approach: Query decomposition breaks complex queries into simpler sub-queries. Step Functions orchestrates multi-stage processing. Look for decomposition, multi-stage, or orchestration in answers.
Keywords: complex, multi-part, decomposition, compound questions

Pattern: "scale or performance"
Approach: OpenSearch with HNSW indexes for fast approximate search at scale. Batch embedding for ingestion throughput. Async processing for non-real-time retrieval.
Keywords: scale, performance, throughput, latency, millions of documents

================================================================================
KEY TAKEAWAYS
================================================================================
1. Chunking strategy directly impacts retrieval—test different sizes with your content
2. Titan Embeddings V2 is the default; evaluate alternatives on your specific domain
3. Hybrid search (keyword + vector) beats pure vector for most production RAG
4. Reranking improves precision significantly but adds latency—use for high-stakes
5. Query optimization (expansion, decomposition, HyDE) bridges the query-document gap

================================================================================
COMMON MISTAKES
================================================================================
- Using only vector search when hybrid would produce better results
- Chunks too large (diluted relevance) or too small (lost context)
- Not evaluating embedding models on domain-specific test queries
- Skipping query preprocessing for user-facing applications
- Assuming retrieval is 'good enough' without measuring recall on test queries
